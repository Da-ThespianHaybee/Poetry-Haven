The Hopeful Negroes

By Abubakar Saadu (Prince Haybee)

Poem

They rise with sun and sleep with pain,
Their dreams like clouds—both soft and vain.
With backs bent low and hearts held high,
They walk beneath a stolen sky.

Their skin, a map of ancient grace,
Yet history shuns their rightful place.
In fields of sweat and silent tears,
They plant the hope of better years.

Chained by laws and ruthless hands,
Still they march across the lands.
In every prayer and whispered song,
A promise that they still belong.

They do not seek a crown or throne,
Just voices that are not alone.
A home, a name, a chance to be—
To dance once more, proud and free.

Poem Synopsis

*The Hopeful Negroes* captures the resilience and endurance of oppressed Black communities, particularly reflecting on historical and modern struggles. The poem is a tribute to generations who have toiled, marched, prayed, and hoped for a future where their dignity is fully recognized. It focuses on their strength rather than victimhood, portraying hope as both their burden and their weapon.

Analysis

This poem mirrors the ongoing racial and social injustices faced by Black individuals across centuries. It connects slavery, systemic racism, and modern civil rights struggles into one poetic narrative. The imagery— “a stolen sky,” “fields of sweat,” “a name, a chance to be”—evokes the depth of deprivation yet refuses to dwell in despair. Instead, the rhythm and rhyme elevate the subject's voice, echoing movements like Black Lives Matter and earlier civil rights marches. It is a powerful reminder that even in marginalization, there is pride, unity, and a relentless pursuit of freedom.
